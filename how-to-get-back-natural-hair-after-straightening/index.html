How To Get Back Natural Hair After Straightening

Written by Aprilia Oct 11, 2023 Â· 4 min read

If you've been straightening your hair for a while and now want to get back to your natural curls, you may be wondering where to start. You may have damaged your hair through heat styling or chemical treatments, but don't worry, it's not too late to restore your hair's natural texture. In this article, we'll show you how to get back natural hair after straightening with easy steps, tips, and FAQ.

Description

Straightening your hair can be a fun way to switch up your look, but it can also be damaging to your hair. Heat styling and chemical treatments can weaken your hair's structure and cause breakage. The good news is that you can get back your natural hair texture with the right care and treatment.

How to Get Back Natural Hair After Straightening: Step-by-Step Guide

Step 1: Stop Using Heat Tools

The first step in getting back natural hair after straightening is to stop using heat tools. Heat styling tools like flat irons and blow dryers can damage your hair and prevent it from reverting back to its natural texture. If you need to use heat styling tools, use them on low heat and apply a heat protectant to your hair.

Step 2: Deep Condition Your Hair

Deep conditioning is essential for restoring your hair's natural texture. Use a moisturizing hair mask once a week to help repair any damage to your hair. Make sure to apply the mask from the roots to the tips of your hair and leave it on for at least 20 minutes before rinsing it off with cool water.

Step 3: Use a Wide-Tooth Comb

Using a wide-tooth comb is essential for preventing breakage and damage to your hair. Start combing your hair from the ends and work your way up to the roots. This will help to detangle your hair without causing any damage.

Step 4: Let Your Hair Air Dry

Air drying your hair is the best way to prevent damage and maintain your hair's natural texture. Avoid using a blow dryer and let your hair air dry instead. You can also use a microfiber towel to dry your hair gently.

Tips to Get Back Natural Hair After Straightening

Tip 1: Get Regular Trims

Getting regular trims is essential for maintaining healthy hair. Trims can help to prevent split ends and breakage, which can make it difficult for your hair to revert back to its natural texture.

Tip 2: Use a Satin Pillowcase

Using a satin pillowcase can help to prevent breakage and damage to your hair. Satin is a smooth material that won't tug or pull on your hair while you sleep, helping to maintain your hair's natural texture.

Tip 3: Avoid Chemical Treatments

Avoiding chemical treatments like relaxers and perms can help to maintain your hair's natural texture. Chemical treatments can weaken your hair and make it difficult for it to revert back to its natural state.

FAQ About Getting Back Natural Hair After Straightening

Q: How long does it take for hair to revert back to its natural texture?

A: It can take anywhere from a few weeks to several months for your hair to revert back to its natural texture, depending on the severity of the damage.

Q: Can I straighten my hair again after getting back my natural texture?

A: Yes, you can straighten your hair again, but make sure to use heat protectant and low heat settings to prevent damage.

Q: Will cutting my hair help it revert back to its natural texture?

A: Cutting your hair can help to remove any damaged ends, but it won't necessarily make your hair revert back to its natural texture.

Hair Care for Natural Hair

Now that you've restored your natural hair texture, it's important to take care of it. Here are some tips for maintaining healthy natural hair:

  • Use a sulfate-free shampoo
  • Use a moisturizing conditioner
  • Avoid using heat styling tools
  • Deep condition your hair once a week
  • Get regular trims
  • Protect your hair from the sun and chlorine

Getting back natural hair after straightening is possible with the right care and treatment. Follow these steps, tips, and hair care routine to maintain healthy, beautiful natural hair.
